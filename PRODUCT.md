# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Women 22–35, urban, smartphone-first. Using Flux during quiet personal moments: morning coffee, post-workout wind-down, end of day. They want to feel understood by their body, not managed by an app. Non-technical; they respond to warmth and clarity, not clinical dashboards or data. Secondary user: a partner who has received an opt-in phase summary link — they see one card, nothing else.

## Product Purpose

Flux makes the menstrual cycle legible and useful in everyday life. It tells you what phase you're in, what that means for your energy, food, and mood today, and gives you one actionable insight. It does not diagnose, does not add anxiety, and does not track for tracking's sake. It replaces the vague awareness of "I'm having a week" with "I'm in the luteal phase — this is normal, here's what helps."

## Brand Personality

Warm, clear, grounded. Not clinical. Not girlboss-pink. Not generic wellness. The app should feel like a knowledgeable friend who happens to understand women's health deeply and speaks to you like an adult.

## Anti-references

- Clue: too clinical, too data-heavy, feels like a medical spreadsheet
- Flo: too pink, too gamified, too fertility-obsessed for non-TTC users
- Generic wellness apps: gradient orbs, vague "namaste" copy, empty animations that delay content
- SaaS dashboards dressed as health apps: charts everywhere, metric obsession, no emotional warmth

## Design Principles

1. Phase context is the product. Every screen should feel clearly anchored in the current phase — color, tone, copy, and advice shift when the phase shifts.
2. One thing at a time. Show the most important thing first. Don't front-load the screen with eight equal-weight cards.
3. Warmth over clinical precision. Friendly, direct voice, generous whitespace, no anxiety-inducing copy phrased as medical warnings.
4. Earned familiarity. Standard patterns (card, tab bar, form controls) are features here, not laziness. Users trust interfaces they already know how to use.
5. Body, not behavior. The app describes what is happening and what helps — it never prescribes, judges, or implies the user is doing it wrong.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. No color-only state indicators (always pair with text or icon). All inputs at 16px to prevent iOS auto-zoom. No viewport zoom restrictions (maximumScale removed).
